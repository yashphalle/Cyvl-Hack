The problem

Somerville's sewers were built in the 1800s. Most streets still carry sewage and stormwater in one shared pipe. When it rains hard, that pipe overflows, and the mix discharges into the Alewife Brook and the Mystic River. These events are called combined sewer overflows, or CSOs.

How a combined sewer overflow happens.

The fix in the $1.29 billion regional plan is sewer separation: dig up the street, replace the one combined pipe with two (sewage in one, stormwater in the other). The plan commits about 103 acres of separation in Somerville’s catchments (8 on Alewife, 95 on Mystic — the 446-acre Charles share is Cambridge and Boston’s), chose storage tanks and tunnels for the rest, and adds conveyance routes and a microtunnel. Add the city’s rolling dig calendar — water mains, utility work, the repairs that already scarred 46.5% of street segments — and Somerville will be opening streets for decades.

Separation replaces one combined pipe with two, and the open trench is a chance to rebuild the street above it.

The gap: the regional plan decides how much to separate per watershed. It says nothing about which specific streets to dig in which order. Somerville just finished a three-year separation in Spring Hill, where the city used the open streets to add bike lanes, raised crosswalks, and rain gardens, all coordinated manually. Highland Avenue is next. Thirty more years of digging follow, and the sequencing is still done by hand.

What we're building

Sewershed is a map tool that ranks every still-combined street in Somerville by separation-readiness: how smart it would be to dig that street next. The ranking comes from joining two real datasets that have never been combined:

  • The city's own sewer GIS — 7 catchment areas, every pipe with its type (2,438 segments are explicitly labeled "Combined"), install dates back to the 1800s, manhole depths.
  • Cyvl's street-level scan — pavement condition every 30 feet, utility-cut patching (46.5% of segments), sidewalk condition, ADA ramps, catch basins, and a street photo of every asset.
Two datasets joined into one score, three outputs a city can act on.

How a street earns its rank

Five weighted factors. Every input is a named field in real data, and the weights are editable in the interface.
Scope boundary: we do not claim CSO volume reductions per street. Separation only pays off once a whole catchment is finished. What we optimize is the order: lowest cost, least disruption, most bundled value per dig.

How it impacts the city

Bundling: when the dig order is chosen well, each excavation fixes several problems at once. Spring Hill proved this works; Sewershed makes it systematic.

Money

Utility cuts measurably shorten pavement life — Los Angeles documented arterial life falling from 25 to 16.5 years, Phoenix measured a 4.5-year loss per cut street. Our modeled envelope: roughly $5–10M on the committed separation work alone — about 7–10% of the street-work segment we actually touch (most of the budget buys tanks and tunnels, where software saves nothing) — and the same bundling engine applies to every street the city opens for any reason. The mechanism: never tearing up fresh asphalt, always digging where repaving was due anyway.

Rivers

The order doesn't change what separation achieves, but it changes how fast catchments complete. Finishing whole catchments sooner brings forward the day each outfall stops discharging into the Alewife and the Mystic.

Accessibility

Somerville's ADA backlog is estimated at $75 million against roughly $1 million a year in dedicated funds. Every separation trench that also installs a ramp or rebuilds a failing sidewalk chips at that backlog with money that was being spent regardless.

Residents

Streets get dug once instead of repeatedly. We test the ranking against 311 complaints: if the streets residents already report for sewer and flooding problems cluster where the model points, the loudest pain gets addressed earliest.

Beyond Somerville

Boston runs the same CSO program on the same rivers, two miles downstream. The method (city pipe GIS plus Cyvl street scan plus a readiness score) transfers to Boston's outfalls without changes. It also slots next to Boston's existing COBUCS coordination system: COBUCS manages when utilities dig, Sewershed decides which streets deserve the dig first.

What the demo looks like

1 · The red city

All of Somerville with every still-combined pipe in red, separated streets in green. One overlaid number: 2,438 segments to go.

2 · Thirty years in ten seconds

Press play and streets flip red to green in optimal dig order while a year counter runs 2026 to 2056 and a savings counter accumulates dollars from coordinated digs.

3 · The evidence card

Click street #1: pavement score 38, pipe installed 1924, shallow cut, failing sidewalk and missing ramp on the block, with the actual Cyvl street photo.

4 · Ask-the-city search

Type "fresh trench patch" and Cyvl's image search returns real Somerville photos in seconds. Overlay 311 complaints to test whether they cluster on our top-ranked streets.

5 · The deliverable

Hit export and open the DXF live in Autodesk Viewer. This is the file a civil-engineering consultant actually receives.

Reliability

Everything runs offline from precomputed data. The only two live-network steps have screen-recording backups.
